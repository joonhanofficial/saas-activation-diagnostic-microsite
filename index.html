01. Headline Metric

The headline activation rate was not the full story.

A stable aggregate activation rate can hide where product adoption is stronger or weaker. The business question was not whether activation existed, but where activation quality changed by acquisition source and plan tier.

02. Method Challenge

From unstable timing logic to a stronger activation rule.

01

Timing logic tested

What it means: Measuring activation through time-to-first-usage logic.

Why it mattered: This is the default approach for many SaaS products, assuming faster usage equals better activation.

02

Timing logic rejected

Why it mattered: A large share of subscriptions showed first usage earlier than subscription start.

Risk solved: Prevented false positives by discarding unreliable sequencing logic.

03

Usage depth summarized

What it means: Shifting focus to actual usage records and distinct feature utilization.

Why it mattered: Reflects deeper product engagement instead of simple login timestamps.

04

Final activation rule selected

What it means: A stricter rule based strictly on engagement depth.

Risk solved: Made the metric more defensible by removing timing artifacts.

05

Segmentation prepared for Tableau

What it means: Building the final subscription-level table exported to CSV.

Why it mattered: Enabled segmented views by referral source and plan tier.

06. Final Interpretation

The useful signal was not the average. It was the pattern underneath it.

1

Source quality shaped activation

Organic and event channels brought in significantly stronger cohorts than paid ads.

2

Plan tier changed the interpretation

Enterprise plans, despite higher value, underperformed the cheaper tiers in pure activation rate.

3

Interaction between source and plan mattered most

Activation quality was not just a product metric. It was shaped by acquisition context and subscription context together.
